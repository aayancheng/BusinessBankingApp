What is a Business Credit Score?

A credit score turns everything we know about a business into one simple number that estimates how likely it is to repay a loan. Here's how ours works — built and tested on a made-up population of small businesses.

1The idea in one minute

Higher score = safer borrower. Lower score = riskier. It's the same idea as a personal FICO score (300–850), but for a company. Slide the control to see how a score maps to a letter grade and to the real repayment risk we observed at that level.

2How the scores are spread out

Each bar is a slice of the 300–850 scale; its height is how many businesses landed there. Colors show the letter grade. Most businesses sit in the middle, with fewer at the very safe and very risky ends — like a real credit population.

3The whole point: a lower score really does mean more risk

This is the test that matters. We group businesses by grade and check how many actually failed to repay. If the score is any good, the bars should fall sharply from grade D to grade AAA — and they do.

4What pushes a score up or down

The score weighs many facts about a business. Longer bars = more influence on the score. The strongest signals are intuitive: can the business cover its loan payments, how maxed-out its credit is, and how much debt it carries.

5See a pattern for yourself

Two examples of how a single fact relates to risk. Businesses that comfortably cover their debt payments (high DSCR) fail far less often; businesses that are nearly maxed-out on credit (high utilization) fail far more often.

About this page. Every number here comes from our actual trained model scoring a synthetic population of businesses — no real or confidential data is used. Model quality: AUC and KS (both standard "how well does it separate safe from risky" measures; higher is better). The score is a Weight-of-Evidence + logistic "scorecard," the long-standing, explainable approach used across consumer and business lending.
